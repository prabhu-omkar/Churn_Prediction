# Why XGBoost Dominates Tabular Churn Prediction: A Mathematical and Empirical Analysis

**Domain:** Machine Learning, Predictive Analytics, Online Gaming Industry  
**Dataset:** Online Gaming Behavior Dataset (N = 40,034 player records, 17 features)  
**Primary Reference:** Chen, T. & Guestrin, C. (2016). *XGBoost: A Scalable Tree Boosting System*. KDD '16. arXiv:1603.02754

## Abstract

Player churn—the permanent cessation of a user's engagement with an online game—represents one of the most costly challenges for game publishers, where acquisition costs routinely exceed retention costs by an order of magnitude. This paper presents a mathematically rigorous investigation into why XGBoost (eXtreme Gradient Boosting) is the superior algorithmic choice for binary churn prediction on structured, tabular behavioral data. We begin from first principles, deriving the XGBoost objective function, its second-order Taylor expansion approximation, the closed-form solution for optimal leaf weights, and the gain-based split scoring criterion. We then contrast XGBoost's formulation against the theoretical limitations of Logistic Regression (a linear hyperplane classifier) and Random Forest (a parallel bagging ensemble). We address the class imbalance problem using SMOTE and justify our metric selection via a formal analysis of the Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC). Finally, we provide SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) game-theoretic attribution analysis grounded in cooperative game theory. Empirical results across four models on a holdout test set of 8,007 samples demonstrate XGBoost's decisive supremacy: **Accuracy = 95.23%, F1 = 0.9064, ROC-AUC = 0.9395, MCC = 0.8745**, outperforming the next best competitor (Random Forest) by +1.52 percentage points in Accuracy and +1.87 points in F1.

## 1. Introduction

The online gaming industry operates on a **freemium engagement economy**: the vast majority of a game's revenue is generated by a small minority of highly engaged players, while the majority form a transient user base with high attrition rates. The term **churn** refers to the event where a player permanently disengages—ceasing to log in, making no further purchases, and becoming lost to the game's ecosystem.

From a business perspective, the cost asymmetry is stark. User acquisition—paid media, install campaigns, social advertising—routinely costs between $2 and $100 per install depending on genre and platform. Retention, by contrast, costs a fraction of that via in-game rewards, push notifications, and personalized offers. Therefore, a model that can accurately flag at-risk players *before* they disengage yields a direct, computable return on investment.

This paper is not merely a benchmark study. We seek to answer a fundamental question: **why, at a mathematical and algorithmic level, does XGBoost emerge as the dominant framework** for this class of problem? The answer lies in:

1. A second-order loss approximation that grants XGBoost unparalleled optimization power compared to first-order gradient descent.
2. Built-in L1/L2 regularization that directly penalizes model complexity—absent in Random Forest.
3. A sequential, additive training paradigm that systematically corrects residual errors—fundamentally different from the parallel averaging of bagging.
4. Sparsity-aware and approximate split-finding algorithms that scale efficiently to tens of thousands of records and dozens of engineered features.
5. Full compatibility with SHAP-based cooperative game-theoretic attribution for trustworthy model explainability.

## 2. Problem Formulation and Dataset

### 2.1 Dataset Characteristics

The dataset comprises $N = 40{,}034$ player records drawn from an online gaming platform. Each record $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ contains $d = 12$ raw features covering:

- **Behavioral metrics:** `PlayTimeHours`, `SessionsPerWeek`, `AvgSessionDurationMinutes`, `AchievementsUnlocked`
- **Economic engagement:** `InGamePurchases`
- **Progression:** `PlayerLevel`
- **Demographic attributes:** `Age`, `Gender`, `Location`
- **Preference attributes:** `GameGenre`, `GameDifficulty`

### 2.2 Target Variable Derivation: Proxy Churn Label

The dataset contains no explicit boolean churn flag—a common characteristic of behavioral logs where disengagement is passive. We operationalize the churn label using the `EngagementLevel` field:

$$y_i = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if EngagementLevel}_i = \text{"Low"} \quad (\text{Churned}) \\ 0 & \text{if EngagementLevel}_i \in \{\text{"Medium"}, \text{"High"}\} \quad (\text{Retained}) \end{cases}$$

**Resulting class distribution:**
- Class 0 (Retained): $N_0 = 29{,}699$ instances ($74.18\%$)
- Class 1 (Churned): $N_1 = 10{,}335$ instances ($25.82\%$)

This yields an **imbalance ratio** of approximately $\rho = N_0 / N_1 \approx 2.87$.

### 2.3 Chi-Square Independence Testing

We validate the independence of categorical features from the churn outcome using the **Pearson Chi-Square test**:

$$\chi^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \sum_{j=1}^{c} \frac{(O_{ij} - E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}}$$

where $O_{ij}$ is the observed cell count and $E_{ij} = \frac{R_i \cdot C_j}{N}$ is the expected count under $H_0$ (feature is independent of churn).

| Feature         | $\chi^2$ Statistic | $p$-value | Decision ($\alpha = 0.05$) |
|:----------------|:------------------:|:---------:|:---------------------------:|
| Gender          | 0.31               | 0.5770    | Fail to reject $H_0$        |
| Location        | 2.64               | 0.4506    | Fail to reject $H_0$        |
| GameGenre       | 4.46               | 0.3469    | Fail to reject $H_0$        |
| GameDifficulty  | 1.93               | 0.3809    | Fail to reject $H_0$        |

**Conclusion:** All $p$-values substantially exceed $\alpha = 0.05$. Demographic and preference-based categorical features carry **zero predictive signal** for churn. Churn is driven entirely by continuous behavioral variables—a finding confirmed by SHAP analysis in Section 13.

## 3. Feature Engineering

Raw behavioral data rarely provides optimal signal-to-noise representation for tree-based models. We engineer **five new features** through domain-informed transformations:

### 3.1 Definitions

$$\text{PlayTimePerSession} = \frac{\text{PlayTimeHours}}{\text{SessionsPerWeek} + \epsilon}$$

where $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$ prevents division-by-zero. Distinguishes *binge* players from *habitual* players.

$$\text{AchievementRate} = \frac{\text{AchievementsUnlocked}}{\text{PlayerLevel} + 1}$$

A low rate indicates potential frustration with the game's difficulty curve—a precursor to churn.

$$\text{TotalWeeklyMinutes} = \text{SessionsPerWeek} \times \text{AvgSessionDurationMinutes}$$

Provides a single absolute engagement volume measure, normalizing qualitative behavioral differences.

$$\text{IsHighLevel} = \mathbf{1}\bigl[\text{PlayerLevel} \geq Q_{75}(\text{PlayerLevel})\bigr]$$

A binary indicator for players in the top quartile of progression, capturing endgame churn dynamics.

$$\text{AgeGroup} = \text{pd.cut}(\text{Age}, \; \text{bins}=[0, 18, 25, 35, 100], \; \text{labels}=[\text{Teen, YoungAdult, Adult, Senior}])$$

Discretizes the continuous age variable into semantically meaningful bins.

### 3.2 Preprocessing Pipeline

After one-hot encoding categorical variables with `OneHotEncoder(drop='first')` (preventing multicollinearity via the dummy variable trap), the effective feature dimensionality expands to $d' = 17$. Continuous features are standardized:

$$x'_{i,j} = \frac{x_{i,j} - \mu_j}{\sigma_j}$$

This is required for Logistic Regression convergence but has **no effect** on tree-based splits, which are threshold-based and invariant to monotonic feature transformations.

## 4. Class Imbalance: SMOTE

### 4.1 The Problem of Majority-Class Bias

A classifier trained on an imbalanced dataset minimizing empirical risk will trivially achieve high accuracy by predicting the majority class. The **trivial majority-class classifier** $f_{\text{trivial}}(x) = 0 \; \forall x$ achieves:

$$\text{Acc}_{\text{trivial}} = \frac{N_0}{N_0 + N_1} = \frac{29{,}699}{40{,}034} \approx 74.18\%$$

This renders raw accuracy misleading and motivates rebalancing.

### 4.2 SMOTE: Mathematical Formulation

SMOTE (Chawla et al., 2002) generates synthetic minority samples by **interpolating** in the feature space rather than duplicating existing points.

**Algorithm:** For each minority-class instance $\mathbf{x}_i \in C_1$:

1. Compute the $k$-nearest neighbors of $\mathbf{x}_i$ within $C_1$ using Euclidean distance in the standardized feature space.
2. Select a neighbor $\mathbf{x}_{\text{nn}}$ uniformly at random.
3. Generate a synthetic instance:

$$\mathbf{x}_{\text{new}} = \mathbf{x}_i + \delta \cdot (\mathbf{x}_{\text{nn}} - \mathbf{x}_i), \quad \delta \sim \mathcal{U}(0, 1)$$

This creates a new point on the **line segment** connecting $\mathbf{x}_i$ and $\mathbf{x}_{\text{nn}}$, geometrically expanding the minority class decision region rather than reinforcing isolated points.

### 4.3 Effect on Training Set

| Split                  | Samples     | Churn Ratio |
|:-----------------------|:-----------:|:-----------:|
| Before SMOTE (train)   | 32,027      | 25.8% / 74.2% |
| After SMOTE (train)    | 47,536      | 50.0% / 50.0% |

> **Critical Note on Data Leakage Prevention:** SMOTE is applied *exclusively* to the training fold. The holdout test set retains the original class distribution to provide an unbiased estimate of real-world performance. Applying SMOTE before the train/test split constitutes data leakage—synthetic minority samples generated from test-set neighbors would artificially inflate minority recall.

## 5. Theoretical Analysis: Gradient Boosting Framework

### 5.1 Additive Training and the Objective Function

XGBoost belongs to the family of **additive tree ensemble models**. The prediction for instance $\mathbf{x}_i$ at boosting round $T$ is:

$$\hat{y}_i = \sum_{t=1}^{T} f_t(\mathbf{x}_i), \quad f_t \in \mathcal{F}$$

where $\mathcal{F}$ is the space of regression trees (CARTs). The model is built sequentially: at round $t$, we seek to add $f_t$ that most reduces the regularized objective:

$$\mathcal{L}^{(t)} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} l\!\left(y_i,\; \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)} + f_t(\mathbf{x}_i)\right) + \Omega(f_t)$$

where:
- $l$ is a differentiable loss function (binary cross-entropy for classification)
- $\hat{y}_i^{(t-1)}$ is the ensemble's prediction *before* adding tree $t$
- $\Omega(f_t)$ is the regularization penalty on tree $f_t$

Unlike gradient descent in parameter space, we optimize in **function space**: at each step, we select an entire tree $f_t$.

### 5.2 Second-Order Taylor Expansion

The inner term $l(y_i, \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)} + f_t(\mathbf{x}_i))$ is difficult to optimize directly. XGBoost performs a **second-order Taylor expansion** around $\hat{y}_i^{(t-1)}$, treating $f_t(\mathbf{x}_i)$ as the perturbation:

$$f(x + \Delta x) \approx f(x) + f'(x)\,\Delta x + \frac{1}{2} f''(x)\,(\Delta x)^2$$

Applying to the loss with $\Delta = f_t(\mathbf{x}_i)$:

$$l\!\left(y_i, \hat{y}^{(t-1)} + f_t(\mathbf{x}_i)\right) \approx l\!\left(y_i, \hat{y}^{(t-1)}\right) + g_i f_t(\mathbf{x}_i) + \frac{1}{2} h_i f_t^2(\mathbf{x}_i)$$

where:

$$g_i = \frac{\partial\, l(y_i, \hat{y}^{(t-1)})}{\partial\, \hat{y}^{(t-1)}} \qquad \text{(first-order gradient)}$$

$$h_i = \frac{\partial^2 l(y_i, \hat{y}^{(t-1)})}{\partial\, (\hat{y}^{(t-1)})^2} \qquad \text{(second-order Hessian)}$$

**Why is the second-order approximation critical?** Standard Gradient Boosted Decision Trees (Friedman, 2001) use only the first-order gradient $g_i$, equivalent to fitting pseudo-residuals. This is analogous to gradient descent in parameter space. By incorporating $h_i$, XGBoost performs a **Newton step** in function space:

- $g_i$ indicates *which direction* to correct the prediction.
- $h_i$ indicates *how large* the correction should be, providing adaptive step sizing proportional to local curvature of the loss surface.

This yields faster convergence and a more precisely located minimum. Removing the constant term $l(y_i, \hat{y}^{(t-1)})$, the simplified objective at round $t$ is:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(t)} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left[ g_i f_t(\mathbf{x}_i) + \frac{1}{2} h_i f_t^2(\mathbf{x}_i) \right] + \Omega(f_t)$$

**For binary cross-entropy loss** $l(y, \hat{y}) = -[y\log\sigma(\hat{y}) + (1-y)\log(1-\sigma(\hat{y}))]$ where $\sigma(\hat{y}) = 1/(1+e^{-\hat{y}})$:

$$g_i = \sigma(\hat{y}_i^{(t-1)}) - y_i \qquad h_i = \sigma(\hat{y}_i^{(t-1)})\left(1 - \sigma(\hat{y}_i^{(t-1)})\right)$$

The Hessian $h_i \in (0, 0.25]$, maximum at $\hat{y}=0$ (maximum uncertainty). Instances where the model is most uncertain receive the largest per-instance weight—XGBoost naturally focuses on hard cases.

### 5.3 Defining the Tree and Regularization

A regression tree $f_t$ maps each instance to a leaf: $f_t(\mathbf{x}) = w_{q(\mathbf{x})}$, where:
- $q : \mathbb{R}^d \rightarrow \{1, \ldots, T\}$ is the **tree structure** (routing function)
- $\mathbf{w} = (w_1, \ldots, w_T)^T \in \mathbb{R}^T$ are the **leaf weight scores**
- $T$ is the number of leaves

The XGBoost regularization term is:

$$\Omega(f_t) = \gamma T + \frac{1}{2} \lambda \sum_{j=1}^{T} w_j^2$$

- **$\gamma T$:** A penalty on the **number of leaves**. Higher $\gamma$ forces fewer leaves, preventing fine-grained partitioning of noise.
- **$\frac{1}{2}\lambda \sum w_j^2$:** An **L2 (Ridge) penalty** on leaf weight magnitudes, shrinking outputs toward zero and preventing any leaf from making an overly aggressive prediction.

XGBoost also supports an **L1 (Lasso) penalty** $\alpha\sum_j|w_j|$ promoting leaf weight sparsity.

### 5.4 Reformulating the Objective Over Leaves

Define the instance set in leaf $j$: $I_j = \{i \mid q(\mathbf{x}_i) = j\}$

Substituting $f_t(\mathbf{x}_i) = w_{q(\mathbf{x}_i)}$ and grouping by leaf:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(t)} = \sum_{j=1}^{T} \left[ \left(\sum_{i \in I_j} g_i\right) w_j + \frac{1}{2}\left(\sum_{i \in I_j} h_i + \lambda\right) w_j^2 \right] + \gamma T$$

Define aggregate leaf statistics:

$$G_j = \sum_{i \in I_j} g_i \qquad H_j = \sum_{i \in I_j} h_i$$

Then:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(t)} = \sum_{j=1}^{T} \left[ G_j w_j + \frac{1}{2}(H_j + \lambda) w_j^2 \right] + \gamma T$$

This is a sum of independent **quadratic functions** in each leaf weight $w_j$, enabling closed-form optimization.

### 5.5 Closed-Form Optimal Leaf Weights (Proof)

The $j$-th leaf's objective contribution $\mathcal{L}_j = G_j w_j + \frac{1}{2}(H_j + \lambda)w_j^2$ is a convex quadratic in $w_j$ (since $H_j + \lambda > 0$ when $\lambda > 0$).

**Differentiating and setting to zero:**

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_j}{\partial w_j} = G_j + (H_j + \lambda) w_j = 0$$

**Solving:**

$$\boxed{w_j^* = -\frac{G_j}{H_j + \lambda}}$$

This is the **optimal leaf weight**—the exact Newton step for leaf $j$.

**Intuition:** The Hessian $H_j$ in the denominator acts as an adaptive learning rate:
- Leaves with high curvature (large $H_j$, e.g., many uncertain instances) receive **smaller** weight corrections.
- Leaves with low curvature (few uncertain instances) receive **larger** corrections.
- $\lambda$ prevents extreme weights when $H_j \approx 0$ (leaf with few instances), acting as a regularizer.

**Contrast with Logistic Regression:** In LR, $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ is estimated globally by iterative solvers. XGBoost computes each leaf's optimal weight *analytically* and *locally*, enabling massively parallel computation across leaves.

### 5.6 Tree Structure Score

Substituting $w_j^*$ back into the objective yields the **minimum achievable loss** for fixed structure $q$:

$$\tilde{\mathcal{L}}^{(t)}\!\left(q\right) = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^{T} \frac{G_j^2}{H_j + \lambda} + \gamma T$$

This is the **structure score**: more negative = better tree. Since $G_j^2/(H_j+\lambda) \geq 0$ always, adding leaves always potentially improves fit. The $\gamma T$ term counteracts this by penalizing leaf count, creating a principled fit-vs-complexity tradeoff.

### 5.7 Split Gain Criterion

Trees are grown greedily: at each node, we evaluate all possible feature-threshold splits. For a split partitioning leaf $j$ into left child $L$ and right child $R$:

$$G_j = G_L + G_R, \qquad H_j = H_L + H_R$$

The **Gain** from this split—the reduction in structure score—is:

$$\boxed{\text{Gain} = \frac{1}{2} \left[\frac{G_L^2}{H_L + \lambda} + \frac{G_R^2}{H_R + \lambda} - \frac{(G_L + G_R)^2}{H_L + H_R + \lambda}\right] - \gamma}$$

**Interpretation:**
- The bracket computes the sum of per-child structure scores minus the parent structure score.
- $-\gamma$ subtracts the leaf penalty for the new leaf.
- If Gain $> 0$: split accepted. If Gain $\leq 0$: split rejected (**pre-stopping**, not post-hoc pruning).

**Contrast with Gini/Entropy (CART):** Standard decision trees use heuristic impurity measures independent of the model's actual loss function. XGBoost's Gain is derived directly from the objective, making it loss-function agnostic and theoretically principled.

## 6. Algorithmic Innovations of XGBoost

### 6.1 Exact vs. Approximate Greedy Split Finding

The **exact greedy algorithm** sorts each feature and evaluates every threshold: $O(nd\log n)$ complexity. Intractable for $n > 10^5$.

The **approximate algorithm** proposes candidate split points using the **Weighted Quantile Sketch**. Each instance $x_i$ is weighted by its Hessian $h_i$ (its importance in the second-order approximation). The rank function:

$$r_k(z) = \frac{\sum_{(x, h) \in \mathcal{D}_k, x < z} h}{\sum_{(x, h) \in \mathcal{D}_k} h}$$

An $\epsilon$-approximate quantile set $\{s_{k1}, \ldots, s_{kl}\}$ satisfies $|r_k(s_{k,j}) - r_k(s_{k,j+1})| < \epsilon$, reducing complexity to $O(nd/\epsilon)$.

### 6.2 Sparsity-Aware Split Finding

Real-world behavioral data is sparse: zero in-game purchases, zero achievements, and one-hot encoded categorical features all introduce structural zeros. For each node, XGBoost learns a **default direction** for missing/sparse entries by testing both directions and selecting the one maximizing Gain. Computation visits only non-missing entries—near-linear in the number of non-zeros.

### 6.3 Block Structure and Cache Optimization

Data is stored in **compressed column (CSC) format** in memory blocks. This layout is reused across iterations, avoiding expensive re-sorting and enabling parallelized split-finding across CPU cores. For out-of-core computation, block-based disk I/O and cache optimization enable training on datasets exceeding RAM capacity.

## 7. Why Logistic Regression Fails

### 7.1 Linear Decision Boundary

Logistic Regression models the log-odds of churn as a linear function:

$$\log\frac{P(y=1|\mathbf{x})}{P(y=0|\mathbf{x})} = \boldsymbol{\beta}^T \mathbf{x} + \beta_0$$

$$P(y=1|\mathbf{x}) = \sigma(\boldsymbol{\beta}^T \mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\boldsymbol{\beta}^T \mathbf{x}}}$$

The decision boundary is $\boldsymbol{\beta}^T \mathbf{x} + \beta_0 = 0$—a **hyperplane** in $\mathbb{R}^d$. This is a fundamental inductive bias: the classifier can only partition feature space into two half-spaces.

**Why this fails for churn:** Player churn exhibits strong **non-linear threshold effects**. For example:
- Churn risk is near-zero when `SessionsPerWeek` $\geq 4$ AND `AvgSessionDurationMinutes` $\geq 30$, but spikes when *both* fall below threshold simultaneously.
- This is a non-linear, conjunctive pattern—impossible to represent with a single linear boundary.

To capture an interaction between two features $x_a$ and $x_b$, Logistic Regression requires the explicit construction of $x_a \cdot x_b$. For $d$ features, there are $\binom{d}{2} = O(d^2)$ pairwise interactions and $O(d^k)$ $k$-th order interactions—making exhaustive manual engineering intractable.

### 7.2 Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Parameters $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ are estimated by maximizing the log-likelihood:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \arg\max_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} \sum_{i=1}^n \left[y_i \log \sigma(\boldsymbol{\beta}^T \mathbf{x}_i) + (1-y_i)\log(1 - \sigma(\boldsymbol{\beta}^T \mathbf{x}_i))\right]$$

This is a **convex optimization** with a unique global minimum—but the convexity that guarantees correctness also confines the model to the linearity assumption. No amount of training data or optimization iterations transcends the hyperplane boundary.

### 7.3 Empirical Evidence

| Metric    | Logistic Regression | XGBoost (Tuned) | Gap       |
|:----------|:-------------------:|:---------------:|:---------:|
| Accuracy  | 84.70%              | 95.23%          | +10.53%   |
| F1        | 0.7501              | 0.9064          | +0.1563   |
| AUC       | 0.9238              | 0.9395          | +0.0157   |
| MCC       | 0.6605              | 0.8745          | +0.2140   |

The gap between AUC (0.9238) and F1 (0.7501) for Logistic Regression is the fingerprint of a model that *ranks* probabilities reasonably well (capturing the linear trend in the data) but fails to *precisely locate* the decision boundary in a curved feature space.

## 8. Why Random Forest is Suboptimal

### 8.1 The Bagging Algorithm

Random Forest constructs $B$ independent trees via bootstrap aggregating:

1. Draw bootstrap sample $\mathcal{D}^{(b)}$ of size $n$ with replacement.
2. Train a deep CART $T^{(b)}$ on $\mathcal{D}^{(b)}$, considering only $m = \lfloor\sqrt{d}\rfloor$ random features per split.
3. Aggregate by majority vote:

$$\hat{y} = \text{mode}\left\{T^{(1)}(\mathbf{x}), \ldots, T^{(B)}(\mathbf{x})\right\}$$

### 8.2 Variance Reduction Without Bias Reduction

For $B$ uncorrelated classifiers each with variance $\sigma^2$, the ensemble variance is $\sigma^2/B$. However, bootstrap samples from the same dataset induce correlation $\rho > 0$ between trees:

$$\text{Var}[\bar{T}] = \rho\sigma^2 + \frac{(1-\rho)\sigma^2}{B}$$

As $B \rightarrow \infty$, this converges to $\rho\sigma^2$—a lower bound governed by inter-tree correlation. Random feature selection reduces $\rho$ but cannot eliminate it.

**Critically, averaging cannot reduce bias.** If each tree is biased toward misclassifying boundary cases (moderate-engagement players), the average will be equally biased. **Bagging is structurally incapable of systematic bias correction.**

### 8.3 Absence of Explicit Regularization

Random Forest's implicit regularization consists only of:
- **Bootstrap sampling:** ~37% of data omitted per tree (out-of-bag).
- **Feature subsampling:** Restricts splits to $m$ features per node.
- **Majority vote:** Cancels individual tree errors.

These heuristic mechanisms provide no principled control over model complexity in the way that $\gamma T + \frac{1}{2}\lambda\|w\|^2$ does. Without $\lambda$, leaf weights can be arbitrarily large; without $\gamma$, there is no direct cost for adding leaf nodes.

### 8.4 No Sequential Residual Correction

Each Random Forest tree is trained independently, with no knowledge of previous trees' errors. Consequently, Random Forest cannot focus its capacity on **hard cases**—borderline players most difficult to classify. XGBoost's sequential architecture reweights the objective at each round so that poorly-fit instances (high $|g_i|$, informative $h_i$) receive more corrective attention.

| F1        | 0.8801        | 0.9064          | +0.0263   |

## 9. The Bias-Variance Decomposition: Boosting vs Bagging

For any prediction function $\hat{f}$, the expected prediction error decomposes as:

$$\mathbb{E}\!\left[(y - \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}))^2\right] = \underbrace{\left(\mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(\mathbf{x})] - f(\mathbf{x})\right)^2}_{\text{Bias}^2} + \underbrace{\text{Var}[\hat{f}(\mathbf{x})]}_{\text{Variance}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{Irreducible noise}}$$

| Property            | Logistic Regression      | Random Forest              | XGBoost (Boosting)              |
|:--------------------|:------------------------:|:--------------------------:|:-------------------------------:|
| Bias                | High (linear constraint) | Low (deep trees)           | Very Low (sequential correction)|
| Variance            | Low                      | Low (bagging average)      | Controlled ($\eta$, $\lambda$, subsampling) |
| Regularization      | L1/L2 on $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ | Implicit (subsampling) | Explicit $\gamma, \lambda, \alpha$ |
| Bias Reduction      | ✗                        | ✗                          | ✔ (residual fitting)            |
| Variance Control    | ✔ (limited)              | ✔ (bagging)                | ✔ (shrinkage, regularization)   |

**Boosting achieves what bagging cannot:** by fitting each new tree to residual errors, boosting reduces model bias in function space. The **learning rate (shrinkage)** $\eta \in (0, 1]$:

$$\hat{y}_i^{(t)} = \hat{y}_i^{(t-1)} + \eta \cdot f_t(\mathbf{x}_i)$$

slows bias reduction while significantly reducing variance by preventing any single tree from dominating the ensemble. This allows a larger $T$ before overfitting occurs.

The optimal combination is: **low $\eta$, large $T$, explicit $\lambda$ and $\gamma$**—precisely the configuration identified by our grid search (Section 11).

## 10. Evaluation Metric Justification

### 10.1 ROC-AUC

The Receiver Operating Characteristic curve plots TPR vs FPR across all thresholds $\tau \in [0,1]$:

$$\text{TPR}(\tau) = \frac{TP}{TP + FN} \qquad \text{FPR}(\tau) = \frac{FP}{FP + TN}$$

$$\text{AUC} = \int_0^1 \text{TPR}(\text{FPR}^{-1}(t)) \, dt = P(\hat{p}(y=1|\mathbf{x}^+) > \hat{p}(y=1|\mathbf{x}^-))$$

AUC equals the probability that a randomly chosen churned player is ranked higher than a randomly chosen retained player—a threshold-independent measure of rank discrimination.

### 10.2 Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC)

For imbalanced binary classification, accuracy is misleading and F1-score ignores True Negatives. **MCC** incorporates all four confusion matrix cells:

$$\text{MCC} = \frac{TP \cdot TN - FP \cdot FN}{\sqrt{(TP+FP)(TP+FN)(TN+FP)(TN+FN)}}$$

MCC equals the Pearson correlation coefficient between observed labels $y_i$ and predicted labels $\hat{y}_i$. Range $[-1, +1]$:
- MCC $= +1$: Perfect prediction
- MCC $= 0$: No better than random guessing  
- MCC $= -1$: Perfect inverse prediction

The trivial majority-class classifier achieves: **Accuracy = 74.18%, F1 = 0.00, MCC = 0.00**. MCC cannot be inflated by predicting only the majority class—it requires simultaneously high TP, TN, and low FP, FN. Chicco & Jurman (2020) demonstrate MCC is the only common single-number summary invariant to class imbalance ratios.

## 11. Hyperparameter Optimization

We performed **Stratified $k$-Fold Grid Search** ($k=3$) optimizing XGBoost hyperparameters. Stratification preserves the original 25.8%/74.2% class ratio per fold.

**Search space:**

| Hyperparameter  | Values Searched | Mathematical Role |
|:----------------|:---------------:|:------------------|
| `max_depth`     | {5, 7}          | Maximum tree depth; limits interaction order $\leq$ depth |
| `learning_rate` ($\eta$) | {0.05, 0.1} | Shrinkage factor; $\eta \to 0$ reduces variance, requires larger $T$ |
| `n_estimators` ($T$) | {200, 300} | Number of boosting rounds |
| `subsample`     | {0.8}           | Fraction of training data sampled per round (stochastic gradient boosting) |

**Optimization criterion:** F1-score. Chosen to balance false negative cost (missed churners—lost retention opportunity) against false positive cost (unnecessary retention spend on non-at-risk players).

**Optimal parameters:** `max_depth=7`, `learning_rate=0.1`, `n_estimators=300`, `subsample=0.8`.

**Effect of `subsample=0.8`:** Stochastic gradient boosting randomly samples 80% of training data per round, decorrelating successive trees and providing variance reduction analogous to bagging—while retaining the bias-reduction advantage of sequential boosting. This is a hybrid mechanism absent in pure bagging or pure boosting.

## 13. Model Explainability via SHAP

### 13.1 Game-Theoretic Foundation: Shapley Values

**SHAP** decomposes each prediction into additive feature contributions:

$$\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}_i) = \phi_0 + \sum_{j=1}^{d} \phi_j^{(i)}$$

where $\phi_0 = \mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(\mathbf{x})]$ is the baseline and $\phi_j^{(i)}$ is the **Shapley value** of feature $j$ for instance $i$:

$$\phi_j = \sum_{S \subseteq \mathcal{N} \setminus \{j\}} \frac{|S|!\,(|\mathcal{N}| - |S| - 1)!}{|\mathcal{N}|!} \left[\hat{f}_{S \cup \{j\}}(x_{S \cup \{j\}}) - \hat{f}_S(x_S)\right]$$

where $\mathcal{N}$ is the full feature set and $\hat{f}_S(x_S)$ is the model's expected prediction when only features in $S$ are observed. The fractional coefficient is the **weighted average** over all orderings of adding feature $j$ to coalition $S$.

This attribution satisfies four axioms from cooperative game theory:
- **Efficiency:** $\sum_j \phi_j = \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}) - \phi_0$
- **Symmetry:** Features with identical contributions receive equal Shapley values.
- **Dummy:** Features that do not change any prediction receive $\phi_j = 0$.
- **Additivity:** Values for combined games equal the sum of individual game values.

These axioms collectively guarantee a *unique*, *fair* attribution—there is no other additive method satisfying all four simultaneously.

### 13.2 TreeExplainer: Polynomial-Time Computation

Naively computing Shapley values requires $2^d$ model evaluations (exponential). For XGBoost's tree ensemble, **TreeExplainer** (Lundberg et al., 2020) computes exact Shapley values in polynomial time $O(TLD^2)$ where $T$ = trees, $L$ = max leaves, $D$ = max depth. By traversing internal tree paths and tracking expected output changes as features are added or removed, TreeExplainer exploits the conditional independence structure of CART trees—achieving exact results without retraining on subsets.

### 13.3 Global Feature Attribution Results

$$\text{GlobalImportance}_j = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} |\phi_j^{(i)}|$$

**Top behavioral drivers of churn (ranked by mean $|\phi_j|$):**

1. **`SessionsPerWeek`:** Dominant predictor. Low session frequency pushes SHAP values strongly positive (toward churn). Sharp threshold at $\approx$ 3 sessions/week.

2. **`AvgSessionDurationMinutes`:** Second strongest signal. Short sessions predict churn orthogonally to frequency.

3. **`TotalWeeklyMinutes`** (Engineered): Captures variance not fully explained by its constituent components—validates our feature engineering.

4. **`PlayTimeHours`:** Low cumulative investment correlates with higher attrition.

5. **`AchievementsUnlocked`:** Sparse achievement progression signals disengagement from the reward loop.

### 13.4 Confirmation of Statistical Tests

SHAP values for `Gender`, `Location`, `GameGenre`, and `GameDifficulty` are near zero across all test instances—confirming the chi-square independence test. The model correctly learns that these features carry no predictive signal.

## 14. Discussion and Business Implications

### 14.1 Why XGBoost is the Theoretically Correct Choice

The online gaming churn prediction task exhibits four properties collectively making XGBoost optimal:

**Property 1: Non-linear behavioral threshold effects.** Churn risk exhibits rapid threshold transitions (not linear decline). XGBoost's depth-$D$ trees capture interactions of order $\leq D$, while Logistic Regression is strictly first-order.

**Property 2: Mixed continuous and discrete feature types.** Tree splits are invariant to monotonic feature transformations, naturally handling the mixture of continuous metrics and categorical features without information loss.

**Property 3: Moderate class imbalance.** The 25.8% churn rate—combined with SMOTE and MCC/F1 optimization—is well-addressed by XGBoost's Hessian-weighted leaf weights, which simultaneously maximize correct positive and negative classifications.

**Property 4: Explainability requirement.** Business stakeholders require actionable retention triggers. XGBoost's compatibility with SHAP TreeExplainer provides exact, game-theoretically grounded attributions per player.

### 14.2 Actionable Recommendations

1. **The 3-Session Intervention Threshold:** SHAP reveals a sharp churn risk increase when `SessionsPerWeek` drops below ~3. Trigger escalating re-engagement sequences (push notifications, daily login bonuses) at this threshold.

2. **Session Quality Monitoring:** Players with `AvgSessionDurationMinutes` below the population median should receive asynchronous content suggestions (new modes, limited-time events) designed to extend individual session length.

3. **Achievement Loop Optimization:** Players with low `AchievementRate` are frustration-proximal. Adaptive difficulty scaling should detect these players and reduce challenge to restore progression momentum.

4. **Demographic-Agnostic Targeting:** Chi-square tests and SHAP jointly confirm that age, gender, location, and genre are irrelevant to churn. Retention budgets should not be segmented demographically—only by behavioral features.

## 15. Conclusion

This paper has presented a comprehensive, mathematically rigorous investigation into the superiority of XGBoost for tabular churn prediction. We derived the complete XGBoost optimization framework from first principles: the additive ensemble objective, the second-order Taylor approximation enabling Newton-step optimization in function space, the closed-form optimal leaf weight $w_j^* = -G_j/(H_j + \lambda)$, the tree structure score, and the gain-based split criterion incorporating explicit $L_0/L_2$ regularization.

We demonstrated theoretically why Logistic Regression—constrained to a linear decision hyperplane—cannot model the non-linear threshold effects governing player attrition; and why Random Forest—a parallel bagging ensemble without explicit regularization or residual correction—is structurally incapable of the bias reduction that distinguishes XGBoost.

Empirically, on 40,034 player records from an online gaming platform, the tuned XGBoost model achieves:

$$\text{Accuracy} = 95.23\%, \quad F1 = 0.9064, \quad \text{AUC} = 0.9395, \quad \text{MCC} = 0.8745$$

outperforming Logistic Regression by **+10.53% in Accuracy** and **+0.2140 in MCC**, and outperforming Random Forest by **+1.52% in Accuracy** and **+0.0368 in MCC**. SHAP analysis confirmed `SessionsPerWeek`, `AvgSessionDurationMinutes`, and `TotalWeeklyMinutes` as principal behavioral drivers, while all demographic features were statistically and informationally insignificant.

XGBoost's dominance is not accidental—it is the direct consequence of a theoretically superior second-order optimization framework, principled multi-term regularization, sequential residual error correction, approximate Hessian-weighted split finding, and full compatibility with game-theoretic explainability. For structured behavioral tabular data with non-linear interactions, mixed feature types, and moderate class imbalance, XGBoost represents the current empirical and theoretical gold standard.
